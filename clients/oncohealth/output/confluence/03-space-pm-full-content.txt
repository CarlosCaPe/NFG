## Document & Content Management System (Quadient)

Background

Oncology Analytics generates over 100,000 documents annually to support the oncology utilization management (UM) product serving health plans.  Determination, case processing and outreach communications are generated using over 200 templates, several hundred content blocks and post-generation editing that is conducted outside of MATIS, separately in Microsoft Word.  The existing document and content management system built within MATIS (aka fax templates and fax contents) is not scalable and is risk-prone as the UM business continues to grow, and we deploy more full-delegation clients which require more diverse notification types (e.g. member denials).  

A comprehensive 4-month vendor selection process resulted in choosing the Inspire product by Quadient to replace our document and content management system.  The implementation project kicked off in June 2020 and is expected to run through the end of 2020.

2. Business Objectives

Provide ability to quickly scale and onboard new health plan clients

Reduce clinician and CRS time spent on communication generation and management

Automate communication generation using details at the case level

Enhance and enforce compliance with communication regulatory requirements by automating compliance approval steps and version control

Standardize and improve maintenance required for clinical content blocks (e.g. denial and RAD language) and outreach content blocks (e.g. request for medical records)

Provide ability to quickly react to regulatory changes and requirements

Standardize communication and templates across health plan clients but still enabling payer-specific requirements and customization (e.g. logos, dynamic content)

Structure template management and reduce template redundancy (e.g. chemo vs XRT vs GMT modules)

Reduce product and engineering resources required to support document management and shift maintenance to operations teams

## Document Attachment Framework

Business Requirements

Oncology Analytics would like to establish a clinical document classification rules framework to identify required documents captured during the prior authorization request process.  The goal is to know with certainty the type of medical records that are uploaded (i.e. labs, pathology, progress note, etc.).  The documents and data recorded in the medical records will be used to increase the efficiency of case processing and facilitate auto approvals. In the future, clinical data captured in the medical records (i.e. lab values, molecular tests, etc.) can be used in conjunction with diagnosis and stage to recommend the appropriate protocols. 

An additional goal is to extract data from the clinical documents to populate the Clinical Data Repository (CDR) which will be used in combination with claims data to create comprehensive, longitudinal data cohorts broken down by diagnosis/cancer type.

NLP Validation

In the current system, there is no validation for the types of documents attached. We only check if a record was blank. If we don&rsquo;t have enough clinical information on the medical records we will send a CCF for More Info Needed. 

The new document management framework will require a new data validation process:  

System feedback during document upload. For example, the system may want to show the required sections of the medical records and what was checked-off or found during the NLP process. 

Provider office feedback during case entry. For example, we may want to prompt the users to confirm they attached the documents that were found by the NLP engine. 

OA clinical feedback during the case review. For example, the OA review confirms the sections/clinical values that are in the attached documents during the case review.

Problem Statement

For our health plans that are now on SmartUM, we are unable to generate accurate Financial Impact reports and are under-reporting our FI by about 85%. These issues have also forced the team to pause the Financial Impact 2.0 rollout.

We estimate that roughly 70% of the FI inaccuracies are due to human error. We believe that by enhancing the SmartUM fields required for FI with appropriate guardrails, we will improve accuracy for our current FI reporting.

### A Review of the Molecular Testing Utilization Management Product

Prepared by Graeme Bolger, 23 June 2020

 Background:

The Oncology Analytics molecular testing utilization management product was initiated in March 2016, with MMM in Puerto Rico (PR) as the lead client. Over the next few years, additional PR clients signed on to the product, including TripleS and First Medical. Scott and White Health plan began submitting cases for review in 2018 and FirstCare in 2019; these 2 organizations have now merged and therefore remain the sole mainland client. Use of the product accelerated in the fall of 2019, but experienced a very significant decline in 2020, related in part to the coronavirus pandemic. Please see the attached PowerPoint documents for case trend volumes over the past 3 years. Further support of the product by Oncology Analytics, if justified at all, needs to reflect these trends.

 

Recent developments in technology:

The molecular testing product is designed to focus on complex molecular tests that are obtained relatively uncommonly (often only once or twice) during a patient&rsquo;s cancer history. These tests are relatively expensive (typically several thousand dollars per test) and are typically available from a relatively small number of test providers. However, these tests are much less expensive than most cancer treatments, especially chemotherapy, which limits the cost savings that can be generated upon utilization review. Although there is extensive technological innovation in the testing space, much of the innovation in the past 2-3 years has led to a lowering of the cost per test. In addition, there is also considerably less patent protection in the molecular test space, compared to chemotherapy, even for relatively expensive and complex tests. These 2 trends, which are likely to persist, have led to a significant lowering of test charges to health plans, especially when molecular testing is bundled with other medical tests. The recent downward trend, or stabilization, in costs, although beneficial to health plans, has limited the value of the molecular testing product to these plans.

 

Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats:

Strengths: A major strength of the molecular testing product is that it draws on the deep clinical expertise at Oncology Analytics, which is especially notable in the area of chemotherapy utilization management. Since many chemotherapy regimens require molecular testing as part of the clinical decision-making process (so-called &ldquo;precision oncology&rdquo;), OA&rsquo;s clinical expertise in chemotherapy review can generate valuable insight on the review of molecular testing cases and thereby provide significant support to the molecular testing product.

Weaknesses: The most important weakness in the product, as it currently exists, is the modest level of cost savings that can be generated. Please see &ldquo;recent trends in technology&rdquo; above, for further details.

Opportunities: There is a considerable opportunity for cross-selling of the molecular testing product to current OA business partners. These clients are typically current and prospective users of the chemotherapy utilization management product, as well as newer OA products, such as Oncology Insights and analytics. The availability of the molecular testing product can reinforce client perceptions that OA is highly capable of providing a broad-based oncology management and analytics platform to its clients.

Threats: There are a number of other vendors that offer UM services in the molecular testing space. In addition, many health care provider networks are moving testing, even complex testing, &ldquo;in-house&rdquo;, where it is less likely to undergo utilization review, or be subject only to review internally. Finally, many test providers are developing the ability to bill for molecular testing on a capitated basis, where individual tests would not be subject to utilization review.

 

OA resources utilized by the molecular testing product:

In 2020, approximately 1-2 molecular testing cases are reviewed manually per day. Please see the attached PowerPoint documents for recent case trend volumes; these include a breakdown of requests between requests that were auto-approved (AUTO) and those that required manual review (NON-AUTO). Test requests are reviewed by an OA medical director six days/week. Currently, one medical director reviews cases five days/week (Monday through Saturday, with Thursdays off), and another medical direct reviews cases one day/week (Thursdays). No support by a PharmD or RN has been utilized to date. CRS support for mainland molecular test cases is excellent, with well-trained team members in place. CRS support for PR cases is variable and would benefit from training of additional CRS members.

              Molecular testing case flows are supported in MATIS by a dedicated module. This module shares significant elements with the standard MATIS logic components that were developed for chemotherapy case flow (e.g., case tracking, intake/eligibility and post-review reporting, etc.). The module also utilizes the same database used for other OA UM products. This database includes a number of key clinical elements used by both the chemotherapy and the molecular testing modules, including a common list of diagnoses (i.e., ICD10 codes). The major differences between the chemotherapy and molecular testing modules are (1) a dedicated dashboard that is used by CRS to enter cases into the molecular test module; (2) a separate set of protocols that are dedicated to testing and PET/CT scan reviews; (3) a dedicated dashboard used by the medical director to enter, edit and update the molecular testing protocols; (4) a separate set of RAD templates.

 

Increasing the efficiency of the molecular testing product:

              Given the relatively low dollar savings generated by the molecular testing product, optimizing the efficiency of the product is essential. At the same time, the current and anticipated low adoption rate of the product by OA clients does not justify undertaking a major and expensive modification of MATIS. Potential areas for improvement might be the following:

Increasing the number of protocols that are AUTO-approved. Many requests processed by the molecular testing product are for relatively inexpensive tests (e.g., Medicare Allowable Cost [MAC] less than $100.00). Over the past year, the protocols for most low-cost tests have been shifted to AUTO-approval. For 2020, 78.8% of all requests were AUTO-approved, which is a higher AUTO-approval rate than for the chemotherapy product (see the attached PowerPoint documents for details). Most of the NON-AUTO tests (21.2% of all requests in 2020) were of significant dollar value (typically, over $1000.00/test). This re-alignment of tests from AUTO to NON-AUTO has improved the efficiency of the test UM process, but it is likely that most of the potential improvements in this area have already been made (i.e., as the only remaining NON-AUTO tests are those with significant dollar value). Therefore, moving forward, it is unlikely that efficiency can be improved significantly by increasing the AUTO-approval rate.

Improving the test assignment page: Most molecular test requests consist of multiple elements (i.e., CPT codes) entered under a common diagnosis (i.e., ICD10). The entry of multiple elements for each test can be time-consuming and error-prone. Therefore, the development of a process for entry of multiple tests simultaneously (i.e., &ldquo;bundles&rdquo;) would reduce CRS/provider time and reduce errors.

Improving the search function: Adding a feature in which multiple fields can be used simultaneously in a protocol/test search (e.g., CPT code, test name, gene name, etc.) would be extremely helpful. These would ideally be searched using Boolean logic (i.e., &ldquo;AND&rdquo;, &ldquo;OR&rdquo;, &ldquo;NOT&rdquo;, etc.). There is proposal that Boolean searches also be used for the chemotherapy product; if such a function were to be added to the chemotherapy product, it should also be adapted to the molecular testing product.

Improving the generation of RADs: The generation of RADs and CCFs in the chemotherapy module is cumbersome and labor-intensive, requiring multiple steps and temporary saving of documents on the reviewer&rsquo;s PC, outside of MATIS. The generation of RADs with the molecular testing module is subject to the same defects, which should be corrected with the same &ldquo;fixes&rdquo; needed for the chemotherapy module.

a.       Similar improvements are needed for the generation of statements or other material that are sent to the payor after a case is approved (i.e., that are used to justify the clinical basis for the approval [guidelines, compendia, etc.]).

Incorporating the decision engine: Currently, the molecular testing product is not able to utilize the decision engine. Incorporating the decision engine into the test module would produce significant flexibility, efficiencies and increased cost savings. An important advantage of this improvement would be the designation of specific protocols as &ldquo;AUTO&rdquo; for certain diagnoses (i.e., ICD10s) and &ldquo;NON-AUTO&rdquo; for other diagnoses. As an example, the test &ldquo;NRAS mutation&rdquo; could be made AUTO for the diagnoses &ldquo;colon cancer&rdquo; and &ldquo;rectal cancer&rdquo; but NON-AUTO for all other diagnoses. In addition to diagnoses, payor-specific rules and other parameters could also be incorporated.

### Humana Dose Rounding and Preferred Drug Steerage

Problem Statement

The cost of cancer care in the United States is expected to exceed $246 billion in 2030.  This represents one of the fastest-growing costs in health care, and oral prescription drugs in the initial and continuing phases of medical care are a primary driver[1]. 

Utilization management strategies that focus on authorizing treatments based on evidence-based care only miss opportunities to:

&middot;       Guide towards clinically equivalent lower cost alternatives such as biosimilars

&middot;       Manage waste of high cost drugs through dose rounding and vial optimization.

Communicating health plan preferences to providers in a timely and transparent way is a key factor to implementing successful cost-effectiveness strategies and reducing provider abrasion.

Opportunity

Since 2015, the FDA has approved 28 biosimilars, of which 16 are for anti-cancer and supportive care agents.  Biosimilars have the potential to reduce the spend of high-cost biologic medicines by up to 40%[2].  However, widespread adoption of biosimilars has been slow for various reasons, including provider and patient awareness, concerns over interchangeability and immunogenicity and incorporation into clinical practice.  Within oncology, steerage towards generics and biosimilars have a significant potential to reduce healthcare costs and increase a patients' access to novel treatment options.

Vial optimization strategies present significant opportunities to reduce drug waste. The Hematology/Oncology Pharmacy Association (HOPA) recommends rounding drug doses to the nearest vial size to minimize drug waste, ensure accuracy during drug preparation, and reduce health care expenditures. Dose rounding is especially relevant for drugs that are supplied in single-use vials in a preservative-free formulation. HOPA recommends dose rounding of biologic and cytotoxic anticancer treatments using an amount of 5% or 10% to reduce product wastage, will produce annual cost savings.

Enhanced Drug Steerage Proposal

Oncology Analytics has developed technology to drive targeted drug guidance and implement cost-effectiveness strategies during the ePrior Authorization process. The module will automate guidance to health plan preferred drug policies and suggest evidence-based vial size recommendations during case entry into the Oncology Analytics ePrior Authorization portal.

The integrated technology will give providers ease of use of the Humana medical drug policies by prompting the provider&rsquo;s office to select a preferred alternative instead of a non-preferred drug or enter medical justification for requesting non-preferred alternative. Examples include prioritizing genetic therapies, prioritizing biosimilar therapies and prioritizing preferred agents when medications have a similar mechanism of action.  

The module will require the provider practice staff to enter the height, weight and select the requested dosing regimen, which will prompt evidence-based recommendations based on Humana policies and HOPA guidelines.  Recommendations can be configured for select drugs.  The module will address and mitigate waste by calculating patient specific dose and suggesting optimal dose rounding and vial sizes. The recommendations for the prescribing provider will attempt to steer to preferred drugs or dose rounding and will capture their reasons if they choose not to follow the recommendations.   

This module will allow Humana greater flexibility to implement cost-effectiveness strategies and quickly make changes to preferred drug preferences, all while notifying providers in a transparent manner during the ePrior Authorization process. Integrating technology-enabled drug steerage and vial optimization into the workflow will reduce the number of provider outreach events including Clinical Consultation Forms (CCFs) and Peer-to-Peer consultations, ultimately reducing provider abrasion.

On a monthly basis, Humana reports will be delivered through Oncology Insights (analytics platform) to monitor progress and measure success of the program.  The analytics package will include provider performance metrics on dose rounding and preferred drug adoption (e.g. Biosimilar Adoption Rate) as well as reconciliation of claims with Oncology Analytics total billable units and/or vial recommendations.

### Humana: 2023 SOC Step Therapy Bypass (Rituxan Hycela & Herceptin Hylecta)

Requirements still in progress

Business Case:

In 2023, the Humana policy team is trying to integrate lower cost sites of care into their clinical review policies. They will be updating their PA policies for Rituxan Hycela & Herceptin Hylecta in the Commercial LOB to allow a bypass of Step Therapy for these drugs if the provider attests that they will be administered in the home setting for subsequent doses. These drugs must be given alone or with other oral drugs and not part of a clinical trial for the SOC policies. These policies changes will be effective on 01/01/2023. Humana is currently enforcing their SOC policies retrospectively after receiving claims that indicate the drug was administered at a more expensive outpatient hospital facility.

OncoHealth will not be intervening on the SOC policies nor issuing any kind of partial approval. OH's role will be to capture the provider's attestation that subsequent doses will be administered in the home setting and flag cases that meet this criteria in Humana's auth file so Humana's SOC Concierge Team can work with the member and provider to change the SOC.

Project Timeline:

Background: OH informed Humana that these workflows will not be live on 01/01/2023. OH will be rolling out the Site of Care feature with Health Partner's SmartUM go-live (date: TBD). The infrastructure used to implement HP's SOC workflow can also be used for Humana's. Therefore, we intend to offer a 2 phase approach:
 

Phase 1:  Use existing SmartUM functionality to send a courtesy email to Humana indicating that a Humana Commercial case has one of the two impacted drugs. The case would still be reviewed by OH to prevent decrease in potential financial impact.

Phase 2: Full SOC feature workflow. Step therapy rules would be bypassed based on provider's attestation response. OH would review the case and make final determination. SOC cases would be flagged on Humana's auth file.

### Technical Design for Outbound Interactive Voice Response (IVR)

Overview

The Interactive Voice Response (IVR) Automation project is aimed at automating outbound call activities, allowing the CRS team more time to process cases. Implementing an automated Interactive Voice Response System (IVR) solution reduces the number of manual phone calls made to health plan members regarding PA decisions, extends the mailing timeframe if contact is made with members, and decreases the number of case status calls managed by CRS​.

Key Requirements

Provide an API that can be leveraged by consumers, mainly (MATIS), to initiate call requests to members

Build a dynamic Interactive Voice Response (IVR) system that can be leveraged by registered payers

Maintain Contact Log Record (CLR) of outbound calls to members about approval decisions 

Hight-Level Architecture

IVR High-Level Architecture

Solution

The trigger event for outbound call into IVR Solution:

CRS Member clicks Call Member button

The request is Queued in a Service Bus Topic &ldquo;ivr-outbound-call-queue&ldquo;

The request is picked up by an Azure Function Service Bus Trigger

IVR Orchestration Service obtains Contact Information such as (Phone Number, First, Last name etc)

IVR Orchestration Service dispatches a call to Twilio IVR Client passing required data 

UI

The Call member button will be presented in the Process Assignment screen whenever one or more approved protocols exist for the case.

Database

MATIS DB should keep track of Contact Call Logs for any calls initiated by the CRS member

IVR Orchestration Service shall keep track of call requests and create Contact Log Records (CLR)

Security

There are no security changes for the MATIS UI to schedule a call request for a member

The Azure Function collection will be secured using the default authentication mechanism provided by Microsoft and by using a &ldquo;Function Key&ldquo;, which the IRV Client (Twilio) must include in the header when consuming this API.

Authorization scopes
Function: This Key applies only to the specific function under which it was defined. When used as an API key, it only allows access to that function.

### White Bagging - 2022

MAPD White Bagging Definition for 2022 - Medicare

 

White Bagging. This is practice where a medication is ordered by a physician, and is supplied pursuant to a prescription, by a specialty pharmacy, who then causes the medication to be delivered directly to the provider&rsquo;s office for administration to the patient in the clinical setting.  The distinguishing factor of this practice is that the medication is delivered directly by the pharmacy to the provider&rsquo;s office. https://www.frierlevitt.com/articles/emerging-trends-in-payor-mandated-white-bagging/ 

 

White Bagging &ndash; Sent directly to provider office from the specialty pharmacy will be treated as a Part D benefit for 2022. Currently treated a Part B and will be put on Part D to catastrophic coverage. Proactive outreach to members and providers.
